# *saudade* — a Portuguese word that became a digital nomad newspaper

The word is older than the country.

It first appears in writing around the 13th century, in the cancioneiros — songbooks — written in Galician-Portuguese. The men were on ships. The women were not. The word means *the love that remains*.

> "A presença da ausência."
>
> *The presence of an absence.*

There is no English word for it. "Longing" is too soft. "Nostalgia" is too sentimental. "Homesickness" is too literal. *Saudade* is the weight you carry for a place that may never have you back.

I named a newspaper after it.

## What it has to do with digital nomads

Every nomad I know carries three or four cities. They left Seoul at 23, took a job in Berlin, burned out in Bali, are now editing from Lisbon. They have a favourite café in each, an apartment they almost stayed in, a friend who drifted away when the visa ran out. They do not call this *saudade*. But it is.

The four numbers a nomad watches every morning are the four edges of the same feeling:

- **how many days the visa permits**
- **how long since the last entry to a tax country**
- **when the health insurance pauses**
- **when the pension residency files**

These are practical. They are also small grief calendars. Every count is a measure of distance from somewhere.

I wanted a newspaper that felt like the calendar — that felt like the count — without pretending to be optimistic about it. Most digital-nomad apps speak in the voice of a productivity coach. *Maximise! Optimise! Hack!* That voice is, frankly, exhausting in a country whose public transport you don't yet understand.

So instead the masthead is a Portuguese word for slow grief, and the newspaper files three city items six days a week, and Sunday is silence — by design.

## What the word costs as a brand

I'll be honest about the trade-offs.

**Search is hard.** "Saudade" is a common Portuguese noun. There are records, fado bars, a Norah Jones song, restaurants in 40 cities. Anyone who hears the name and tries to find me types five letters and gets 11,000,000 results. I show up around page eight.

**Pronunciation is harder.** /sɐwˈðaðɨ/ in Portugal, /sawˈdadʒi/ in Brazil. Anglophones say "sah-DAH-day" and that's already wrong but acceptable. Koreans say 사우다지. Japanese: サウダージ. The word survives every mouth, but no one is sure they're saying it right, so they don't say it out loud, so they don't tell their friends about the app, so the brand spreads slower.

**Trademarking it is impossible.** It's a common word. I can register the wordmark — the specific italic Fraunces lockup — but not the meaning. Any other product can call itself Saudade tomorrow.

I knew all of this before I started.

## Why I did it anyway

Three reasons.

First, the word is *correct*. Most product names are stretched-on metaphors. *Slack* (slack-jawed?). *Notion* (an idea?). *Stripe* (a band of colour?). They mean nothing about their products. *Saudade* means exactly what the product is for. A nomad who reads my masthead understands the brief in five letters. That is rare.

Second, the word is *protected by being unfindable*. The price of bad SEO is the privilege of being the only honest result. If you arrive at saudade.app, it's because someone you trust sent it to you, or because the etymology page caught you reading. Neither of those is a paid acquisition channel.

Third, the word is *gentle*. Most of my competitors are loud. Their marketing screams about freedom and digital nomadism and 100% remote and crypto-funded life. I do not believe any of them. *Saudade* is the opposite vibe. The newspaper says: *you're not winning. You're carrying. We will count the days for you.*

That voice cost me something on growth. It bought me everything on retention.

## The five definitions

In the etymology page on the site I list two. Here are five, for those of you reading.

1. **Folk:** the love that remains.
2. **Linguistic:** /sɐwˈðaðɨ/, feminine noun, from Latin *solitas* — solitude.
3. **Manuel de Melo, 1660:** "*Bem que se padece, e mal que se goza.*" — A good one suffers, an evil one enjoys.
4. **Aubrey Bell, 1912:** "*Saudade is the love that remains after someone is gone… which now triggers the senses and makes one live again.*"
5. **The newspaper, 2026:** "A slow newspaper for people who keep moving."

That last definition is mine. The first four are not. I cite them on the etymology page because a brand built on a word older than the country has an obligation to carry the word's earlier readers with it.
